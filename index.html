Biochemistry · Pre-Dental  —  raeshal.jha@example.com

Hello — I'm Raeshal, a second-year Biochemistry student at the University of California, Riverside, on the pre-dental track. I'm fascinated by how the chemistry of the human body translates into clinical care, and I'm building my undergraduate years around the long arc toward dental school. Every lab section, shadowing hour, and volunteer shift is part of preparing me to be a thoughtful, hands-on dentist who treats patients as whole people rather than a set of teeth.

My interest in dentistry started young. Growing up I watched my own dentist explain procedures with patience and humor, and I noticed how much that simple courtesy changed the way our family felt about going to appointments. I want to be that kind of provider — technically excellent, but also calm, communicative, and warm. Biochemistry felt like the right path because it sits exactly where I want to live as a future clinician: at the intersection of molecular detail and human experience, where understanding enzyme kinetics or the chemistry of saliva and enamel directly informs how I'll one day treat decay, periodontal disease, and oral health overall.

Inside the classroom, I've completed general and organic chemistry, introductory biology, and physics, and I'm currently working through biochemistry and microbiology. My favorite course so far has been organic chemistry — the puzzle-solving feel of mechanisms scratched the same itch I get from a really good logic problem, and it gave me a vocabulary for thinking about how drugs, materials, and biological molecules actually interact. I keep a meticulous study system: handwritten notes, weekly review sessions with a small study group, and a running list of "concepts that confused me" that I revisit before every exam.

Outside the lecture hall, I shadow at a local general dentistry practice and volunteer with a community dental outreach program that brings basic oral-health screenings to underserved neighborhoods around Riverside. Those hours have been some of the most formative of my college career. I've watched a dentist coach a nervous child through their first cleaning, helped an elderly patient fill out paperwork in two languages, and seen firsthand how access to preventive care — or the lack of it — shapes lives. Those experiences are why I'm committed to oral-health equity as a long-term part of my career, not an afterthought.

I'm also a peer tutor for general chemistry and a member of the Pre-Dental Society on campus, where I help coordinate guest speakers and DAT-prep workshops. I love being in the room when a tutee finally sees why an SN1 reaction goes the way it does, or when a freshman realizes their pre-dental timeline is more achievable than it felt the week before. Teaching has made me a much better student: explaining a concept out loud is the fastest way to find the gaps in your own understanding.

Looking ahead, my plan is to apply to dental school after my senior year, with a focus on programs that emphasize community dentistry and patient-centered care. Long-term, I'd love to practice general dentistry in an underserved area, partner with public-health programs to bring screenings into schools, and eventually mentor pre-dental students who are walking the same path I am now. If you're a dentist, a researcher, a fellow pre-dental student, or anyone who'd like to connect, please reach out through any of the links below — I'd love to hear what you're working on.

05 — Interests

Off the clock.

Outside of the lab and the lecture hall, I'm a long-distance runner. I started running during the pandemic as a way to clear my head between Zoom classes, and these days I aim for a half-marathon every spring. Running has taught me almost everything I needed to learn about studying for college science classes: show up on the days you don't want to, build slowly, and trust that small consistent efforts compound into something you couldn't have done at the start.

I also love art and design, especially detail-heavy work like Indian classical embroidery, miniature painting, and architectural sketching. The patience and fine-motor focus those require feel like they belong to the same family as the hand skills I'll eventually need as a dentist — slow, steady, attentive work where small choices matter, and where rushing tends to be the most expensive mistake.

I cook constantly. My current rotation includes my grandmother's dal, a slow-roasted vegetable curry, and an embarrassingly large variety of dumplings I've taught myself to fold. Feeding people is the easiest love language I know, and cooking for friends during finals week has gotten me through more than one rough quarter.

Finally, I read widely about public health and the social side of medicine. I'm especially interested in how oral-health disparities track with broader health-equity issues, and how community-based dentistry can be a meaningful piece of the answer. I keep a running list of articles to share with friends in my pre-dental cohort.
